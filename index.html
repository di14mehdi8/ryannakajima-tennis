About Ryan

Ryan Nakajima grew up on Portland's public courts and went on to play collegiate tennis at Portland State University. After his college career, he turned to coaching full-time so the next generation of Portland players could get the kind of structured, technical instruction that's hard to find outside of a varsity program.

His coaching is direct and fundamentals-first: clean footwork, balanced contact, repeatable patterns, and the competitive habits that hold up under pressure. Whether you're picking up a racquet for the first time or preparing for sectionals, sessions are built around your goals and tracked across lessons so you can see the progress.

Ryan is a native Portlander and coaches across the metro area year-round (indoor in winter, outdoor when the rain finally stops).

Frequently asked questions

Who is Ryan Nakajima?
Ryan Nakajima is a tennis coach based in Portland, Oregon, and a former collegiate tennis player at Portland State University. A native Portlander, he offers private lessons, group clinics, and junior development across the Portland metro area.
Where do lessons take place?
At public and private courts throughout the Portland metro area, including Portland, Beaverton, Lake Oswego, Tigard, Hillsboro, Milwaukie, and West Linn. Indoor courts are used in the rainy season.
How much does a tennis lesson cost?
Private lessons start at $85 per hour. Group clinics are $45 per player for 90 minutes. Five-lesson packages are $385 (a 10% discount). Court fees are included.
Does Ryan coach complete beginners?
Yes. Beginners spend the first few lessons on grip, footwork, and the four core strokes — forehand, backhand, serve, volley — before moving to live-ball and point play.
What ages does Ryan teach?
Juniors from age 6 through high school, college-bound players, and adults of all ages. Junior pathways follow USTA development guidelines.
Do you offer a trial lesson?
Yes. New students can book a discounted first lesson to confirm the coaching style is a fit before committing to a package.
What's your coaching philosophy?
Fundamentals first, then tactics. Clean footwork, balanced contact, repeatable patterns, and competitive habits that hold up under pressure.
